This is a brilliant observation of harmonic psychology. You have pinpointed exactly why the Major Scale works the way it does: it is a battleground between two chords fighting over the same unstable interval‚Äîthe tritone.
You are correct that in a standard Major key, the Dominant 7th (V^7) and the Half-Diminished 7th (vii^{\phi 7}) are the only two naturally occurring seventh chords that contain a tritone.
Here is a breakdown of why they feel so emotionally distinct‚Äîthe "Optimist" vs. the "Nihilist"‚Äîdespite sharing the same "devil's interval."
1. The Optimist: V^7 (Dominant 7th)
The Sound of "Almost Home"
You call this optimistic because the V^7 is teleological‚Äîit has a clear purpose and a destination. It is the engine of Western tonal music.
 * The Anatomy: In the key of C Major, this is G7 (G - B - D - F).
 * The Tritone's Job: The tritone here sits between the major 3rd (B) and the minor 7th (F).
 * Why it works: The tension here is directional. The leading tone (B) desperately wants to go up to C, and the 7th (F) wants to slide down to E.
 * The Feeling: It is "optimistic" because it implies a solution. It creates tension, yes, but it promises that relief (the Tonic chord) is just one step away. It is confident anxiety.
2. The Nihilist: vii^{\phi 7} (Half-Diminished)
The Sound of "Lost at Sea"
The vii^{\phi 7} (often notated as vii7b5) is the dark twin. It contains the exact same tritone as the V^7, but the context changes everything.
 * The Anatomy: In the key of C Major, this is Bm7b5 (B - D - F - A).
 * The Tritone's Job: The tritone here is between the Root (B) and the flattened 5th (F).
 * Why it feels Nihilistic:
   * Root Instability: Unlike the V^7, which is grounded by a strong perfect 5th interval (G to D), the vii^{\phi 7} has no perfect fifth. Its foundation is rotten. It feels like standing on a trapdoor.
   * Weak Resolution: While it can resolve to the Tonic (C Major), it does so weakly. It lacks the "dominant root movement" (falling by a fifth) that gives the V^7 its power.
   * The "Minor" Shadow: The inclusion of the minor 3rd (D) and minor 7th (A) gives it a melancholic quality that the major-sounding V^7 lacks.
If the V^7 asks a loud question ("Where is home?"), the vii^{\phi 7} whispers a rhetorical one ("Does home even exist?").

To see why they feel so different, look at what surrounds the tritone (B-F) in both chords.
